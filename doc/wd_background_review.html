1. What Is a White Dwarf?

When a star with initial mass below roughly 8 M☉ exhausts its hydrogen fuel, it expands into a red giant, expels its outer envelope as a planetary nebula, and leaves behind a hot, dense remnant — the white dwarf. A typical WD has a mass of ~0.6 M☉ compressed into a volume roughly the size of Earth (radius ~0.01 R☉ ≈ 1 REarth). It has no nuclear fuel source; it simply cools over billions of years, fading from Teff ~100,000 K (freshly formed) to Teff ~4,000 K (several Gyr old). The Sun will follow this path in ~5 Gyr.

WDs have a well-understood mass-radius relation (from quantum electron degeneracy pressure), a well-measured cooling sequence (from Gaia photometry and spectroscopy), and precise distances from Gaia parallaxes. This makes them among the most physically characterizable stars in the Galaxy — a fact TWIRL exploits: the WD radius is predictable to ~5–10% from Teff + parallax alone, which is much better than for main-sequence stars and constrains expected transit durations before a search even begins.

The total number of WDs in the Milky Way is ~1010. Gaia has now detected ~1.3 million candidates within ~3 kpc, with ~359,000 high-confidence objects (Gentile Fusillo et al. 2021).

2. Evidence That WDs Have Planetary Systems

2a. Atmospheric Metal Pollution: The Smoking Gun

The atmosphere of a hydrogen-atmosphere (DA) WD is one of the most physically simple objects in astrophysics. Because WD surface gravity is ~105 times Earth's, any element heavier than hydrogen or helium sinks out of the photosphere on timescales of days (for warm DA WDs, Teff > 20,000 K) to millions of years (for cool helium-atmosphere DB WDs). This means that if you observe calcium, magnesium, silicon, or iron in a WD's atmosphere, it was accreted recently — the star is currently swallowing rocky material.

Zuckerman et al. (2003) was among the first to demonstrate the statistical reality of this: 25% of DA WDs showed calcium absorption in their sample. Koester et al. (2005) and subsequent surveys extended this to larger samples. By the time large spectroscopic surveys became available, the consensus fraction settled at 25–50% of all WDs showing detectable heavy element pollution.

Key conclusion (Koester & Wilken 2006; Jura 2006): The accreted material has a bulk composition resembling rocky solar system bodies — it is not solar-abundance interstellar medium material. Calcium-to-silicon and magnesium-to-silicon ratios in polluted WD atmospheres match bulk Earth and primitive chondrites, not comets or the ISM. The most natural explanation: disrupted rocky planetesimals — asteroid analogues — are being tidally shredded as they pass close to the WD, forming a debris disk, and then accreting onto the photosphere.

This is a profound result: it proves that rocky planetary material — the building blocks of Earth-like planets — commonly survives to the WD phase and continues orbiting, and in many cases being scattered into close-in orbits where it is destroyed.

2b. Dusty Debris Disks: The Reservoir

Jura (2003) predicted that tidally disrupted asteroids would form compact, flat, optically thick dusty rings within the WD's Roche radius (at R ~1 R☉ from the star, where tidal forces exceed self-gravity for a rocky body). These disks emit infrared excess — they absorb WD UV/optical light and reradiate it in the near- to mid-infrared.

Spitzer Space Telescope observations confirmed this: infrared excess consistent with compact dusty disk emission is found in ~1–4% of WDs. The disks have temperatures of ~800–1500 K and orbital radii of 10–50 WD radii — precisely within the Roche radius. More recently, WISE and Herschel have expanded the sample.

Key finding: Every WD with a detected dusty disk is also metal-polluted. The causal chain is clear — disk → accretion → photospheric pollution — but the origin of the disk material (disrupted asteroid analogues) was confirmed observationally only with the discovery of WD 1145+017 (see Section 3).

2c. Gaseous Emission Lines: Active Accretion

In the most recently accreting (or most actively accreting) systems, gaseous circumstellar Ca II (and occasionally Fe II, O I) emission lines are detected in optical spectra, rotationally broadened to widths consistent with Keplerian orbits at 10–50 WD radii. The gaseous and dusty components are spatially co-located. These systems are almost certainly in the midst of active rocky body disruption events.

3. Known Transiting/Occulting Objects

3a. WD 1145+017: Disintegrating Minor Planet (Vanderburg et al. 2015, Nature)

Discovery: Andrew Vanderburg and colleagues were searching K2 Campaign 1 data for transiting objects around WDs. WD 1145+017 (Teff = 15,900 K, G ≈ 17) showed periodic, asymmetric dips with an unusual property: at least six distinct periods clustered between 4.50 and 4.93 hours, each producing dips up to 40% depth.

Physical interpretation: The dipping periods correspond to orbital radii just outside the WD's Roche radius. The asymmetric dip profiles — slow ingress, sharp egress — are the signature of a comet-like debris tail trailing a disintegrating body. In other words: a rocky minor planet (or several fragments) is orbiting close to the WD, experiencing tidal and thermal stress, losing mass as a dust/gas cloud, and the cloud periodically transits across the WD. Because the bodies are actively losing mass, each transit looks slightly different from the last.

Corroborating evidence: The WD is independently known to be metal-polluted (Ca, Mg, Al, Si, Fe, Cr, Ti detected spectroscopically), and shows an infrared excess from a circumstellar debris disk. WD 1145+017 is therefore the first system where all three components of the pollution-disk-transit chain were confirmed simultaneously in a single object.

"We report the discovery of a disintegrating minor planet transiting a white dwarf… The minor planet may have been scattered inward from a stable reservoir by gravitational perturbations from a surviving planet, and is now being tidally disrupted… Our detection of this system confirms the disrupted minor planet model for metal-polluted white dwarfs and debris disk systems." — Vanderburg et al. (2015)

Follow-up: The system has been studied intensively (Rappaport et al. 2016, Gänsicke et al. 2016). The dip structure is highly variable: some transits disappear for months, new periods appear. At least one body orbiting at a ~4.5 h period is consistent with a rocky fragment ~1–30 km in size, losing mass rapidly (mass-loss rate ~108–109 g/s). This system alone proves that rocky body transits are detectable in WD light curves with the right photometric cadence.

Limitation of K2 detection: The K2 30-minute cadence undersampled the dip structure. Some dips were 10–20 minutes wide. Follow-up high-speed photometry showed much richer dip morphology invisible to K2.

3b. WD 1856+534: The First Giant Planet Candidate (Vanderburg et al. 2020, Nature)

Discovery: TESS Sector 14–15 (2-minute cadence) data on WD 1856+534 (Teff = 4,710 K, G ≈ 17.1) showed periodic, flat-bottomed transits with:

  • Period: 1.4078 days
  • Depth: ~56% (in flux) — consistent with a Jupiter-sized object transiting an Earth-sized star
  • Duration: ~8 minutes
  • Shape: box-like (flat bottom, no limb-darkening signature expected since WD disk is uniform)

The transit geometry: A Jupiter-radius body (R ≈ 11 REarth) transiting an Earth-radius star (RWD ≈ 0.013 R☉ ≈ 1.4 REarth) produces ~(11/1.4)2 ≈ 60% depth — consistent with the observed ~56%. The companion is at orbital radius a ≈ 0.02 AU (P = 1.4 days), well inside where the host star's red giant envelope extended during the AGB phase.

JWST confirmation (2024/2025, Mullally et al.): Mid-infrared secondary eclipse photometry with JWST MIRI measured the companion's thermal emission. Teff = 186 K, mass < 6 MJup (from brightness temperature + evolutionary models). This is now the coldest exoplanet with a directly measured temperature. The companion is almost certainly a giant planet (< 13 MJup), not a brown dwarf.

The dynamical mystery: WD 1856+534 is ~5 Gyr old. During the host star's red giant/AGB phase, the stellar radius expanded to ~1 AU — far beyond the companion's current 0.02 AU orbit. The companion cannot have formed at its current location. It must have migrated inward after the star became a WD. Current mechanisms being explored: high-eccentricity migration via Kozai-Lidov secular oscillations driven by a wide stellar companion (two M dwarf companions are known at ~1500 AU separation), or planet-planet scattering that placed the companion on a highly eccentric orbit that then circularized via tidal dissipation.

"We report a giant planet candidate, WD 1856 b, which transits a cool white dwarf every 1.4 days. The planet is about the same size as Jupiter… It must have migrated to its current orbit after its host star became a white dwarf." — Vanderburg et al. (2020)

Why WD 1856b matters for TWIRL: It is the proof of concept. A 56% transit, 8-minute duration, P = 1.4 days — exactly the signal TWIRL's 200s cadence was designed to detect. It is the benchmark object. Every pipeline decision (detrend, aperture, search statistic) should be validated against recovering WD 1856b before being trusted.

4. Prior Transit Surveys and Their Fundamental Limitation

4a. Faedi et al. (2011)

An early photometric survey of WDs using ground-based telescopes. Small sample, limited coverage, no detections. Pre-TESS.

4b. van Sluijs & van Eylen (2018, MNRAS) — The K2 WD Survey (ADS)

Sample: 1,148 white dwarfs observed by K2 (the extended Kepler mission), using 30-minute long cadence data.

Result: No transiting companions detected. Upper limits on the occurrence rate of Earth-sized and larger companions with orbital periods of 4 hours to 10 days: η = 25–95% (90% confidence).

"We find no convincing transit signals and place upper limits on the occurrence rates of companions around white dwarfs… We place an upper limit on the occurrence rate of Earth-sized companions of 28% for orbital periods shorter than one day, and 96% for periods up to ten days." — van Sluijs & van Eylen (2018)

The structural limitation this survey could not overcome: 30-minute cadence means a transit of duration less than ~30 minutes has at most one data point during transit — effectively undetectable. A WD companion at P = 1 day has a transit duration of roughly 8 minutes (as observed for WD 1856b). At 30-minute cadence, that transit is a single data point at most, indistinguishable from noise. The survey was essentially blind to the WD 1856b class of signal. This is not a criticism — 30-minute cadence was the only cadence available from K2 for most fields — it is simply a fundamental limitation.

This is the single most important context for understanding TWIRL's motivation. The entire prior transit survey literature at scale has been conducted with cadence that cannot detect the dominant expected class of WD transit signals.

4c. Barber et al. (2022)

A survey using TESS 30-minute Full Frame Image photometry. Again, cadence-limited for short-duration transits. No detections at the population level.

4d. Robert et al. (2024, MNRAS) — TESS + High-Speed Survey of Metal-Polluted WDs (ADS)

Sample: 313 metal-polluted WDs observed with TESS (primarily 2-minute cadence where available, else FFI), supplemented with targeted high-speed photometry (ULTRACAM, ULTRASPEC) for the highest-priority targets.

Design philosophy: Rather than surveying all WDs blindly, this work prioritized the metal-polluted subset — the targets most likely to have ongoing accretion and therefore most likely to show transiting debris.

Result: Transit fraction = 0.8% (+0.6/−0.4%, 68% confidence) for dusty or gaseous disk-bearing polluted WDs. They recover two known transiting debris systems in their sample (ZTF J0328-1219 and WD 1054-226). No new giant planet candidates.

"We find a transit fraction of 0.8 ± 0.5 per cent for our sample of 313 metal-polluted white dwarfs… Our result is consistent with the scenario that giant planets approaching the white dwarf via high-eccentricity migration are tidally disrupted or engulfed, rather than surviving to short periods." — Robert et al. (2024)

TWIRL's relationship to this work: Robert+2024 had 313 targets and mixed cadence. TWIRL will have ~359,000 high-confidence WDs, uniformly observed at 200s cadence from Sector 56 onward. The sample size improvement is ~1000×. Even if the transit fraction is only 0.1%, TWIRL expects O(100) detectable systems if they exist.

5. The Theoretical Picture: Planet Survival After Stellar Evolution

5a. What Happens During the Giant Phases?

When the host star leaves the main sequence and expands into a red giant (RGB) and then asymptotic giant branch (AGB) star, the stellar radius grows to 0.5–2 AU at RGB tip and 1–5 AU at AGB tip (depending on stellar mass). Any planet orbiting within these radii faces tidal drag and is spiraled into the stellar envelope — it is engulfed and destroyed.

Mustill & Villaver (2012, ApJ) — the canonical planet survival calculation:

"We find that a planet orbiting inside ~3 AU of a 1 M☉ star (or inside ~5 AU of a 5 M☉ star) will be engulfed during stellar evolution… Terrestrial planets inside 1.5–3 AU are expected to enter the stellar envelope. Low-mass planets with initially wider orbits can survive if their orbits expand due to stellar mass loss. Eccentric terrestrial planets are more at risk of engulfment than circular ones." — Mustill & Villaver (2012)

The key mechanism that allows survival: As the star loses mass (through stellar winds, primarily during the AGB phase, when the star loses roughly half its total mass), planets respond to the reduced gravitational potential by expanding their orbits. A planet at initial radius a0 ends up at approximately af ≈ a0 × (Minitial / Mfinal) after the mass loss. For a 2 M☉ star that leaves a 0.6 M☉ WD: af ≈ a0 × 3.3. A Jupiter at 3 AU moves to ~10 AU. This actually saves planets that were outside the stellar envelope from being engulfed.

The gap: WD 1856b, at 0.02 AU around a 0.52 M☉ WD, cannot have formed or survived at its current location during the giant phases. Something moved it there afterward.

5b. High-Eccentricity Migration

The leading mechanism for post-giant-phase migration to short periods is high-eccentricity migration, likely driven by:

  1. Kozai-Lidov secular oscillations: A companion on a misaligned outer orbit (stellar binary companion, or outer surviving planet) exerts secular gravitational torques that drive the inner planet to high eccentricity. At high eccentricity, the planet's pericenter dips inside the WD's tidal radius, and tidal dissipation in the planet damps the eccentricity while shrinking the orbit. Over time the orbit circularizes at short period.
  2. Planet-planet scattering: Dynamical instability during or after stellar evolution can scatter one planet to high eccentricity, which then migrates via tidal circularization.

For WD 1856b: the two M dwarf companions at ~1500 AU separation are the most likely Kozai-Lidov perturbers. This is supported by the architecture but not yet confirmed quantitatively.

The rocky body disruption mechanism (Jura 2003; Veras et al. 2015): The same secular dynamics that scatter large planets to high eccentricity also scatter smaller rocky bodies (asteroid analogues). When these smaller bodies reach orbits that bring them inside the WD's Roche radius (~0.7–1 R☉), tidal forces shred them. The resulting debris ring circularizes, creates the dusty disk, and accretes onto the WD atmosphere. This is a much more efficient process than giant planet migration — rocky bodies are small and more easily scattered. Hence: pollution is common (25–50%), dusty disks are ~1–4%, transiting debris (WD 1145+017 analogues) are ~0.1–1%.

6. The Gaia Revolution: The Statistical Opportunity

Gentile Fusillo et al. (2021, MNRAS) — The Gaia EDR3 WD Catalog

Data: Gaia EDR3 photometry (G, GBP, GRP) + parallax, selecting stellar objects consistent with the WD cooling sequence in the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.

Method: A probabilistic WD classification (PWD) based on a combination of absolute magnitude, color, and color-magnitude diagram position, trained on SDSS spectroscopically confirmed WDs.

Result: ~1.3 million WD candidates in the full catalog; ~359,000 high-confidence (PWD > 0.75, G ≤ 20) within the Gaia completeness limit.

"We present a catalogue of white dwarf candidates selected from Gaia Early Data Release 3… We identify 1,280,266 high-confidence white dwarf candidates… We estimate the sample to be about 80 per cent complete for PWD > 0.75 within 100 pc." — Gentile Fusillo et al. (2021)

What this means for TWIRL: Before Gaia, the largest spectroscopically confirmed WD catalogs contained ~30,000 objects (SDSS-based). Gaia increased the photometrically selected WD sample by a factor of ~40. For a transit survey, which requires photometric (not spectroscopic) observations, this immediately translates to ~40× the number of targets available. Paired with TESS 200s cadence (Sector ≥ 56), this creates the opportunity for the first statistically powerful transit survey that is not cadence-limited.

7. Why 200s TESS Cadence Is the Threshold Requirement

This is worth stating precisely, because it is the entire basis for TWIRL's existence.

Transit duration for a WD companion:

t_transit = 2 R_WD / v_Keplerian = 2 R_WD / (G M_WD / a)1/2

For a 0.6 M☉ WD with RWD = 0.013 R☉ ≈ 1.4 REarth:

The 30-minute surveys (K2, van Sluijs & van Eylen 2018) are structurally unable to detect WD 1856b-like signals. At 30-minute cadence, the transit of WD 1856b falls in a single time bin — the flux change is diluted to ~56%/9 ≈ 6% over the 30-minute window, and is indistinguishable from systematic noise.

At 200s cadence, a WD 1856b-like transit has 2–3 data points fully inside transit + partial points during ingress/egress. With the ~56% depth of WD 1856b, these points are highly significant even for faint targets (S/N > 10 per point at Tmag < 16 for TESS).

Conclusion: TESS 200s cadence, beginning with Sector 56 (launched 2022), is the first all-sky photometric survey capable of detecting WD 1856b-like signals at statistical rates. No prior survey has had this capability.

8. The Open Questions TWIRL Is Positioned to Answer

Q1: What fraction of WDs have a giant transiting companion at P < ~10 days?

WD 1856b is one confirmed case. Is it rare (η ~ 1 in 10,000) or moderately common (η ~ 1%)? The answer constrains the efficiency of high-eccentricity migration and the survival rate of giant planets through stellar evolution. Even an upper limit of η < 0.01% (from 359,000 non-detections with full completeness) would be the strongest constraint ever placed on this quantity.

Q2: What fraction of WDs have actively disintegrating rocky material in transit?

WD 1145+017 is the archetype. ~10 analogues are known. Is the true occurrence rate ~1%, ~0.1%, or ~0.01%? Given that ~25–50% of WDs are metal-polluted (their rocky material already accreted), the fraction with currently transiting material tells us about the duty cycle of the disruption process. This is a direct test of the disruption models.

Q3: Does the transit/dip rate differ for metal-polluted vs non-polluted WDs?

If rocky disruption causes both the atmospheric pollution and the transiting debris, then polluted WDs should show higher dip rates. A non-detection of correlation would challenge the standard model.

Q4: Does the occurrence rate vary with WD cooling age?

Cooling age (derivable from Teff) is a proxy for time since the WD formed. If companions arrive via tidal circularization, the arrival rate may peak at young WD ages (when the Kozai timescale is shortest). If the occurrence rate is constant or increases with age, that favors a different migration mechanism or a steady-state supply of scattered bodies.

TWIRL's combination of sample size (~1000× prior sensitive surveys) and cadence (200s = transit duration sensitive) is unique. It is not an incremental improvement — it is the first survey that can actually measure the occurrence rate of WD 1856b-like systems with statistical power.

The expected scientific output, even in a null result, is the best published constraint on:

  • Giant planet survival through stellar evolution
  • Post-main-sequence planetary system architecture
  • The connection between rocky debris transits and atmospheric metal pollution

With a detection, TWIRL would produce the second (or more) confirmed transiting giant planet around a WD, enabling comparative planetology of the post-main-sequence system class.
